A Minute Ten

The air was cold and filled with the slightest smell of chlorine. I stood solemnly as the room fell deadly silent; my heart racing with anxiety. The pool water lapped noisily against the dirty-white tiled gutters as I waited behind the starting block for the officials to start the event. Only two hours earlier, my eyes were filled with tears as I drove away from my Dad’s family for the last time. How was it that I was here, in this swimming meet? I should be with them before they’re gone away for good before the only way I would ever see them is on my phone screen. I could feel the pit in my heart grow a little larger when I realized that they would never see me swim another high school meet again, and would never be in the stands to cheer me on.

The referee walked over to the starter, grabbing the small radio into her hand.

“Step up girls,” she called in her usual monotone voice. I snapped back into reality, slowly lifting myself onto the block. Unsteadily, I lowered my body down to gain some sort of balance on the small white platform. For Dad, I thought to myself quietly.

“Take your mark,” the official sounded, and I tucked my head in between my shoulders to the point where I could see the timers behind me. Beep!

A second after the tone sounded my feet left the block and my head was in the water. My dive felt perfect; no part of my body stung with the usual aftermath of a belly flop. I took my first few strokes, determined to pull myself ahead. I felt the power inside me as I continued pushing through the water. Taking my first breath, I saw the young tan girl who swam to my right slowly fall away from me. I put my face back into the water, watching the shadows of the swimmers around me steadily disappear into the distance as I make my first turn. I was in the lead, but now I had to keep it.

My lungs burned as I completed my first fifty yards; my legs begged me to stop using them and just take it easy. Yet from the screaming crowds of swimmers and parents, I heard something I couldn’t have possibly heard; my father cheering me on. I pushed myself forward, desperate to make him proud. My body ached with determination as I went in for my final turn. The last 25 yards… I had to do this, I had to stay strong. So many times I would die out on this length, but not this event, not tonight. I could hear my coach and team cheering me on as I pass the halfway line on the bottom of the pool; but I only focused on the invisible sounds of my father. Looking out as I take my last breath there was no one around me; just me, the cold blue water, and the varsity coach screaming at me to go faster. I whipped my face into the water and sprinted, clawing at the water as if my life depended on it until I reached the dark black touchpad. My lungs desperately ached for air as I placed my hand onto its smooth, soft surface; my heart racing with both uncertainty and exhaustion.

Looking up at the glowing golden numbers, I couldn’t believe my eyes. There, written as brightly as the sun is on a hot summer day, was my time: a 1:09.96. My mouth let out a scream that was more of a screech of pain than a melody of happiness. I had bested my all time record by two seconds, completed the goal I set out to do since the very first meet. I look up to where my father usually sat in the stands, to find it empty. Yet, even though he was now hundreds of miles away, I could see his jubilant eyes and hear his strong clap as I stared into those vacant bleachers. I had done well, made both him and me proud. Tears of happiness flowed from my face as I climbed triumphantly out of the water; covering my immense smile with my hand as I walk over to hear my coaches’ congratulations. I finally broke a minute-ten.
